Exploring Comics in Photovoice

How a digital storytelling tool helped students reflect on gender-based discrimination.

Summary

This thesis explored how comic-based digital storytelling could complement the Photovoice methodology, especially for sensitive topics such as gender-based discrimination among university students. The project combined questionnaires, a focus group, and usability improvements to the Communics platform.

Context

Traditional Photovoice relies on photography and discussion, but photos alone can be limiting:

  • Privacy concerns may stop participants from sharing certain images.
  • Abstract or emotional experiences are hard to capture with photos.
  • Complex issues risk being underrepresented.

Key Insights

Comics lowered barriers to expression. Students felt safer and more creative when using comic panels compared to photography.

Time efficiency. Creating stories in Communics was faster than taking photos over several days.

Privacy protection. Comics allowed participants to share sensitive experiences without exposing themselves.

“I preferred working with Communics because discrimination often occurs through communication or interaction, which is hard to capture in a single picture.”

Solution

I co-designed lightweight improvements for Communics with the platform’s programmer:

  • Simplified user flow to reduce decision fatigue.
  • Improved usability and customization options.
  • Explored potential of AI to suggest dialogues or visuals.

Impact

Evaluation results showed:

  • Creativity Support Index (CSI): average score of 72, indicating strong creativity support.
  • Usability: UMUX-LITE score above benchmark.
  • Group engagement: Communics visuals were rated clearer and more effective than photos (p = .031).
“Communics felt quicker… in traditional Photovoice, I had to spend about a week taking photos.”

Reflections

Designing for sensitive issues requires balancing usability with emotional safety. I learned that digital storytelling tools can reduce barriers to participation while still enabling meaningful reflection. Participants were excited about AI features, but reminded me that technology should support users, not replace their voices.
