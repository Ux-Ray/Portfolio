Research Goal & Focus

As part of a university course project in qualitative research, the goal of our study was to explore how people perceive safety and trust in the context of future public transportation systems equipped with self-driving technology, specifically autonomous buses. Since ‚Äútrust‚Äù is one of the main non-technological barriers to adoption, our focus was on understanding people‚Äôs concerns and the factors that influence their sense of safety.

Research Challenge

One of the main challenges of this project was that autonomous buses do not yet operate in our city. We could not directly observe them, and most people had never experienced one in real life. To address this, we designed our study around regular bus usage, investigating passengers‚Äô behaviors, opinions, and concerns as a way to approximate how people might perceive autonomous buses.

Research methods

Since autonomous buses are not yet available, we grounded our study in the context of regular bus usage. We conducted observations both inside buses and at bus stops, and complemented them with online research. These observations informed the design of our interviews and focus group sessions, where participants were encouraged to reflect not only on their everyday experiences with regular buses but also to project their expectations and concerns onto the concept of autonomous buses.

1. Semi-structured Interviews

  • Conducted 10 interviews on safety and trust in autonomous buses.
  • Explored perceptions of risk, comfort, and adoption.
  • Outcome: Rich qualitative data with direct quotes.

2. Field Observations

  • Visited buses at different days/times (rush hours, evenings, weekends).
  • Observed body language, seat choice, and interactions.
  • Outcome: Identified patterns (e.g., avoiding front seats, hesitations at boarding).

3. Focus Group

  • Session with 12 participants to discuss collective perceptions.
  • Open discussion around trust, safety, and expectations.
  • Outcome: Validated recurring themes; need for clear communication.

Findings

Our research revealed two main areas of concern when imagining autonomous buses: safety perceptions and the role of the driver.

Safety Perceptions

Participants distinguished between physical safety (risk of accidents) and social safety (interactions with passengers, authority presence). Social safety was the greater concern, with many fearing that removing the driver would reduce their sense of security.

The Role of the Driver

Drivers do more than drive: they handle unexpected events, communicate with others on the road, and reassure passengers. Without them, the bus atmosphere would change, as passengers already create ‚Äúprivacy bubbles‚Äù to cope with shared space.

Why These Findings Matter

Our findings revealed that safety perceptions are shaped not only by technical reliability but also by social context. This has important consequences for how autonomous buses are designed and introduced.

Safety is Social as Well as Technical

Passengers emphasized that feeling safe depends on interactions, authority presence, and shared space , not just the risk of accidents.

Drivers Provide More Than Driving

Beyond technical control, drivers act as communicators, problem-solvers, and sources of reassurance. Their absence would alter the social atmosphere of the bus.

Design Implication

For autonomous buses to gain trust, they must recreate this sense of social safety ‚Äî balancing reliable technology with features that support reassurance, order, and comfort in shared travel.
