When photos weren’t enough: exploring comics to support expression in Photovoice

Photovoice is a participatory method that uses photography and discussion to explore social issues. This study investigated how comic-based digital storytelling could expand its expressive potential, particularly for sensitive topics such as gender-based discrimination among university students. The approach was evaluated through questionnaires, a focus group, and usability enhancements to the Communics platform.

What if photography limits what can’t be seen or safely shown?

Through a literature review, I identified several limitations of traditional Photovoice:

  • Participants may avoid taking or sharing certain photos due to privacy concerns or risk of exposure.
  • The method introduces selection bias, favouring images of subjects that are easier to capture while underrepresenting complex or abstract issues.
  • Emotionally complex or abstract or verbal experiences are difficult to represent visually through photography alone.

These observations raised key questions that shaped the starting point of my study, prompting me to evaluate the potential of Communics, both for enhancing individual and collaborative expression and to explore the value of integrating new features such as AI

Role and Skills: Research Analyst — Literature review, critical analysis, ethical awareness, translation into design requirements.

Tools: Mendeley, Google Scholar

When photography fell short, storytelling stepped in

To address key limitations of Photovoice, I explored an existing platform called Communics, a comic-based storytelling tool. With the help of the platform’s programmer, I co-designed lightweight improvements and tested them in a within subject study, in which participants engaged in both traditional Photovoice and Communics-based storytelling. Usability and creativity support were assessed using CSI, UMUX-LITE, and qualitative interviews. Participants were also invited to a focus group, where a Photovoice expert facilitated the session.

How comics made individual self-expression and co-design solutions easier in Photovoice

Recruiting participants for this study was not straightforward. The multi-phase design required repeated commitments, the timing overlapped with students’ exam period, and the sensitivity of the topic added another layer of difficulty. Despite these obstacles, a total of 12 participants engaged in the study, contributing across its different phases.

1. Individual Task – Evaluating Communics

Quantitative data were analysed in R. The evaluation indicated that Communics can support Photovoice by offering a more accessible, flexible, and emotionally safe form of self-expression. The platform achieved a mean Creativity Support Index (CSI) score of 72, reflecting strong support for creativity, and a UMUX-LITE usability score above the benchmark, suggesting good overall usability.

Open-ended questions were analysed thematically. Based on participants’ responses, comic-based storytelling was perceived as more creative, efficient, and less intimidating than photography, especially for abstract or emotional topics. The step-by-step flow reduced decision fatigue, while the AI assistant provided participants with greater freedom in how they presented their stories.

2. Group work task: co-designing solutions with Communics

Organizing the focus group required multiple reschedulings due to participant drop-outs and the facilitator’s cancellation; eventually, 8 students were able to participate.

Results / Achievements:

  • Inspired Ideas: Group storytelling sparked richer concepts and deeper discussions.
  • Design Opportunities: Feedback highlighted the need for improved usability, customization, and real-time collaboration.
  • Clarity Advantage: Communics visuals were rated significantly clearer and more effective than traditional photographs during discussion (p = .031).
  • AI Potential: Participants showed strong interest in AI-generated suggestions (e.g., dialogue or visuals) to lower creative barriers while keeping the reflective process user-driven.

Role: Coordinator & Data Analyst — Participant recruitment and session organization, quantitative analysis (R), qualitative thematic analysis of open-ended responses, synthesis of findings.

Tools: R, Google Forms, Excel

What this project taught me

Working on this project taught me that designing for sensitive social issues is not only about usability, but also about emotional safety and inclusivity. I realized that traditional methods like Photovoice can unintentionally exclude participants, and that digital storytelling tools can lower these barriers while still enabling meaningful reflection.

I also learned the importance of balancing innovation with user needs: while participants were excited about AI support, their feedback reminded me that technology should empower users rather than replace their voices.

Finally, this project showed me how crucial it is to translate user struggles into design opportunities. Every usability challenge or hesitation was a chance to rethink the platform and create a more supportive environment for expression and collaboration.
